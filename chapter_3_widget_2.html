Understanding Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is our tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information in a way that confirms our preexisting beliefs or hypotheses. It's one of the most pervasive cognitive biases humans exhibit.

In Wason's classic 2-4-6 task:

  • Participants tried to discover the rule for number sequences
  • Most tested only examples that fit their hypothesis (like "increasing by 2")
  • They rarely tested sequences that might disprove their hypothesis
  • As a result, many never discovered the actual rule ("any ascending numbers")

This demonstrates how we naturally seek information that confirms what we already believe while neglecting to test alternative possibilities - even when testing alternatives would be more informative.

Confirmation bias affects many areas of our lives:

  • Echo chambers on social media where we only see views similar to our own
  • Medical diagnoses where doctors may focus on evidence supporting their initial impression
  • Political discourse where we discount evidence that contradicts our viewpoint
  • Scientific research where scientists might unconsciously design studies to confirm their hypotheses

To overcome confirmation bias, actively look for evidence that could prove your hypothesis wrong. This scientific approach leads to more accurate beliefs and better decisions.
