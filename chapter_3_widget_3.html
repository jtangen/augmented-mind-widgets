Understanding the Task

In 1966, psychologist Peter Wason developed a task that reveals how our minds approach logical reasoning.

You'll be shown four cards. Each card has a letter on one side and a number on the other. Your job is to verify whether a rule is being followed.

"If a card has D on one side, then it must have 3 on the other side"

This experiment helps reveal confirmation bias - our tendency to look for evidence that confirms our beliefs rather than evidence that could disprove them.

Understanding Confirmation Bias in Logical Reasoning

The Wason Selection Task reveals how our minds naturally approach testing rules, and why confirmation bias leads most people to make predictable errors.

Why Card D?

We must check if D has 3 on the back. If it doesn't, the rule is violated since it states "If D, then 3."

Why Card 7?

We must check if 7 has D on the back. If it did, it would violate the rule since any D must have 3 (not 7) on the other side.

Why not Card K?

The rule says nothing about other letters. The rule is "If D, then 3" - not "If not D, then not 3." Whatever is on K's other side cannot violate our rule.

Why not Card 3?

The rule doesn't say all 3s must have D. It only says all Ds must have 3. Finding a 3 with a letter other than D on the back doesn't violate the rule.

The Power of Confirmation Bias

Most people (over 90% in studies) select Card D (correct) and Card 3 (incorrect). This demonstrates confirmation bias: we naturally look for evidence that confirms our hypothesis rather than evidence that could falsify it.

Checking Card 3 feels intuitive because it seems to "confirm" the rule if it has D on the back. But logically, finding a 3 with any letter other than D doesn't violate the rule.

Meanwhile, Card 7 is crucial for falsification but often overlooked. This reveals how our minds aren't naturally programmed for formal logic, but rather for "positive testing" - looking for confirming instances.

Real-World Impact

This tendency to seek confirming evidence while neglecting potential falsification affects many areas:

  • Scientific research: focusing only on supporting evidence rather than rigorously testing hypotheses
  • Medical diagnosis: confirming initial suspicions while missing critical disconfirming symptoms
  • Political beliefs: seeking news that supports our views while dismissing contradictory information
  • Business decisions: gathering data that supports a strategy without considering evidence against it
